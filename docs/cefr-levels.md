# CEFR levels — quick reference

The **Common European Framework of Reference for Languages (CEFR)** is a widely used scale for describing what learners can do in a language. It has **six levels**, grouped into three bands: **Basic** (A), **Independent** (B), and **Proficient** (C).

---

## Overview

| Level | Band | Label (typical) |
|-------|------|------------------|
| **A1** | Basic user | Breakthrough |
| **A2** | Basic user | Waystage |
| **B1** | Independent user | Threshold |
| **B2** | Independent user | Vantage |
| **C1** | Proficient user | Effective operational proficiency |
| **C2** | Proficient user | Mastery |

---

## A1 — Breakthrough

**Can do:** Very simple, concrete tasks if the other person speaks slowly and helps.

- Understand and use familiar everyday expressions and very basic phrases aimed at satisfying immediate needs (greetings, numbers, simple questions).
- Introduce themselves and others; ask and answer simple personal questions (where they live, people they know, things they have).
- Interact in a simple way **if** the partner is willing to repeat or rephrase slowly.

**Typical limitation:** Vocabulary and grammar are minimal; communication breaks down outside highly predictable situations.

---

## A2 — Waystage

**Can do:** Routine matters in simple, direct exchanges.

- Understand sentences and frequently used expressions related to **areas of most immediate relevance** (personal and family information, shopping, local area, employment).
- Communicate in simple routine tasks requiring a **direct exchange of information** on familiar topics.
- Describe in simple terms aspects of background, immediate environment, and matters in areas of immediate need.

**Typical limitation:** Still struggles with abstract topics, fast speech, and unfamiliar accents without support.

---

## B1 — Threshold

**Can do:** Cope with most situations likely to arise while travelling; produce simple connected text on familiar topics.

- Understand the **main points** of clear standard input on familiar matters regularly encountered in work, school, leisure, etc.
- Deal with most situations likely to arise **while travelling** in an area where the language is spoken.
- Produce **simple connected text** on topics that are familiar or of personal interest.
- Describe experiences and events, dreams, hopes, and ambitions; briefly give reasons and explanations for opinions and plans.

**Typical limitation:** May lack precision and fluency on complex or specialized subjects; errors occur but meaning usually remains clear.

---

## B2 — Vantage

**Can do:** Interact with a degree of fluency and spontaneity that makes regular interaction with native speakers **possible without strain** for either party.

- Understand the **main ideas** of complex text on both concrete and abstract topics, including technical discussion in their field.
- Interact with fluency and spontaneity that makes **regular conversation** with native speakers quite possible.
- Produce **clear, detailed text** on a wide range of subjects and explain a viewpoint on a topical issue, giving advantages and disadvantages.

**Typical limitation:** Nuance, idiomatic depth, and very specialized or literary registers may still be weaker than a native speaker.

---

## C1 — Effective operational proficiency

**Can do:** Use language **flexibly and effectively** for social, academic, and professional purposes.

- Understand a **wide range** of demanding, longer texts and recognize **implicit meaning**.
- Express themselves **fluently and spontaneously** without much obvious searching for expressions.
- Use language **flexibly and effectively** for social, academic, and professional purposes.
- Produce **clear, well-structured, detailed text** on complex subjects, showing controlled use of organizational patterns, connectors, and cohesive devices.

**Typical limitation:** Small gaps compared to educated native speakers may appear in low-frequency vocabulary or rare idioms.

---

## C2 — Mastery

**Can do:** Understand virtually everything heard or read; summarize from multiple sources; express themselves **precisely** in complex situations.

- Understand with ease **virtually everything** heard or read.
- Summarize information from **different spoken and written sources**, reconstructing arguments and accounts in a coherent presentation.
- Express themselves **spontaneously, very fluently, and precisely**, differentiating finer shades of meaning even in more complex situations.

**Typical limitation:** At this level, differences from highly educated native speakers are minor and often context-dependent (e.g. highly specialized jargon).

---

## How levels are often used

- **A1–A2:** Everyday survival, basic customer-facing roles with scripts, short stays abroad.
- **B1–B2:** Many **workplace** roles requiring independent communication, higher education entry in some systems, professional email and meetings.
- **C1–C2:** **Professional** and **academic** environments with demanding language (negotiations, policy, research, public speaking).

---

## Note on assessment

Formal exams (e.g. Cambridge, IELTS, TOEFL) and institutional programs often **map** their scores or outcomes to CEFR bands. Exact mapping varies by provider; always check the official **correspondence table** for the test or certificate you use.
